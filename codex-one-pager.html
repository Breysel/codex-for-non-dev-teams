B3S internal proposal

Help non-developer teams adopt Codex through real workflows

A product-led enablement format: start with obvious wins, use them to collect better stakeholder examples, and teach Codex only where it is the right tool.

30-Second Pitch

Many colleagues hear “Codex” and assume it is only for developers. That is partly true: Codex is a coding agent. But the Codex app can also work with files and data, help prepare decks, reports, interactive pages, and flowcharts, inspect results, and support repeatable workflows. Product can translate between the tool, the business process, and the stakeholder’s actual work.

Why Now

A clear takeaway from the recent Beats & Bytes AI Month was that AI transformation works best when enablement reaches the people doing the work. This proposal follows that principle: start with concrete workflows, help practitioners try Codex safely, then scale the patterns that actually work.

Why Product Should Lead

  • Product managers understand both tool capability and stakeholder context.
  • They can separate valuable workflow automation from novelty demos.
  • They are used to defining inputs, constraints, success criteria, and review paths.
  • They can turn early experiments into repeatable internal patterns.

Why Non-Developers Should Care

  • Codex can help analyze spreadsheets and exports without manual copy-paste.
  • It can help create slide-ready summaries, reports, checklists, and simple tools from structured requirements.
  • It can make recurring operational work more consistent.
  • It can help teams describe automation needs clearly before engineering is involved.

Proposed Format

Session 1: obvious wins and examples

45-60 minutes. Start with broadly useful examples: slides, interactive pages, flowcharts, spreadsheet analysis, recurring reports, handoff checks, and workflow automation. Then give participants time to sketch one task from their own work.

Preparation: turn examples into follow-ups

Select the clearest participant examples, use approved real workflows where permitted, create sanitized sample files when needed, and prepare prompts that show inspect, transform, and verify behavior.

Session 2: hands-on workflows

Demonstrate two or three approved real or sanitized workflows. Participants learn how to write tasks with inputs, constraints, output format, and acceptance criteria.

Follow-up: office hours

Help teams convert promising workflows into reusable prompts, templates, lightweight tools, or engineering-ready briefs.

Success Metrics

Adoption quality

Number of teams with at least one practical, reviewed Codex workflow.

Operational value

Hours saved, error reduction, or reduced handoff friction in repeated work.

Safety maturity

Teams can explain when not to use Codex and how to review Codex results before sharing or acting.

Risks

  • People use Codex for generic chat and see no benefit.
  • Teams try risky data before rules are clear.
  • Codex results are accepted without human review.
  • Early demos feel technical and alienating.

Mitigations

  • Start with obvious wins, then collect concrete use cases.
  • Use approved real data or sanitized examples for demos.
  • Require evidence, file references, and review criteria.
  • Frame Codex as delegation of structured work.
